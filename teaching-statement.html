Teaching Statement

My approach to teaching is shaped by the same question that drives my research: how does experience produce and facilitate learning? In the classroom, as in the laboratory, I work from the premise that knowledge is constructed—that students learn by actively encountering problems, making predictions, and revising their understanding in light of evidence. My background spans cognitive psychology, psycholinguistics, and quantitative methods, with a particular grounding in the experimental design that behavioral research demands, and I have taught across these areas at multiple levels: as the instructor of record for an undergraduate course in language acquisition, as a teaching assistant running hands-on lab sessions in probability and statistics for the social sciences, and as a teaching assistant leading discussion sections in phonology and upper-level seminars in speech science.

Having moved through the educational system in mainland China before pursuing graduate study in the United States, I have learned firsthand how much of academic success depends on norms that are rarely stated outright. The classrooms I grew up in placed great value on foundational rigor and careful preparation. By contrast, the seminars I entered as a graduate student ran on open debate, where forming and defending a position out loud mattered as much as arriving at the right answer. Navigating that shift myself made me acutely aware of the “hidden curriculum”—the tacit expectations around participation, office hours, and asking for help—and I now make these expectations explicit for my students rather than leaving them as unspoken assumptions. Specifically, I explain what office hours are actually for, demonstrate how to approach an intimidating research article, and treat help-seeking as a skill to be taught. This matters especially at a Hispanic-Serving Institution (HSI) like my alma mater, UC Irvine (UCI), where many students, like me, are the first in their families to attend college. I work to build a classroom where students bring their diverse linguistic and cultural backgrounds into our scientific discussions, treating their lived experience with language as an asset in the study of language science. I have seen how powerful this can be: one of my undergraduate research assistants, a heritage-language speaker, brought that lived experience to a project on heritage language, and a personal connection they might have been tempted to leave at the lab door instead became a genuine scientific asset.

Making Research Learnable

My central teaching commitment is to make the practice of research learnable by breaking it into concrete skills and progressively transferring responsibility to students. As a teaching assistant for an upper-level Special Topics in Speech Science seminar, I designed and led independent “research 101” sessions on the tacit skills coursework tends to assume but rarely teaches directly—for example, how to read a primary research article, and how to run and interpret introductory statistical analyses. As a teaching assistant running the lab sessions in probability and statistics, I helped students move from formulas on the page to hands-on analysis of data. And in journal clubs with my research assistants, I deliberately alternate between papers central to my own work and ones chosen around each student's interests, so that learning to read critically stays tied to questions they already care about. Across these settings the principle is the one that opens this statement: students come to understand how knowledge is made by taking part in making it, with support calibrated to where they are.

This same philosophy shapes how I approach generative AI—and it is also where research training pays dividends for the many students who will never set foot in a lab. Rather than treating these tools as something to ban, I see teaching students to use them critically and ethically as part of training competent researchers, and as part of a broader literacy every student needs: the habit of treating a confident-sounding claim as something to be checked rather than accepted. With my own research assistants, I have already worked to demystify these tools—showing where they help, such as scaffolding code or troubleshooting errors, while insisting that the researcher remains responsible for theoretical and analytical rigor. In the experimental-methods courses I am preparing to teach, I plan to build this in directly: students will treat AI-generated output as a hypothesis to be tested and evaluate its reasoning as they would any other source—a skill that serves the future researcher and the future citizen equally.

Mentoring and Research Training

Some of the most durable learning I have witnessed happens when students get their hands dirty with research—and not only the students who go on to become researchers. Mentoring research assistants is one of the most rewarding aspects of my career, and I take my responsibility as a research mentor seriously by being fully involved in every step of their projects. An important part of my job as a mentor is to gauge my students' abilities and aspirations and provide them with tasks that match—offering challenges and room to grow while allowing them to contribute to my broader research goals. One recurring challenge is that undergraduates often begin with hesitation, unsure of how to navigate the ambiguities of authentic research. To address this, I implement a structured, hands-on approach. For example, I closely mentored an undergraduate research assistant, Jacob, beginning with acoustic extraction and TextGrid generation. By providing targeted support and progressively more complex tasks, he grew into an independent researcher who led one of our collaborative projects to a presentation at the Acoustical Society of America (ASA) conference and has since entered a graduate program at UC Davis.

This same structured, hands-on philosophy guided my work as a mentor in an Experimental Methods seminar, where I led two undergraduates through a complete research project, from preregistering their hypotheses through data analysis to a final presentation. Watching them take ownership of a study they had initially found bewildering reinforced my conviction that even early-stage students can carry out authentic research when the process is broken into stages and responsibility is transferred gradually. In effect, I have already served as the senior mentor in a model of vertical mentorship—pairing graduate mentors with undergraduate research teams so that responsibility and expertise cascade down the experience ladder—which I am eager to formalize and scale. I aim to continue fostering this kind of collaborative, lab-based environment to facilitate cooperation and self-guidance among students.

Courses Prepared to Teach

I am prepared to teach across the linguistics and language-science curriculum, from broad general-education courses that may be a student's only encounter with the field to specialized graduate seminars. I can readily step into a department's existing offerings—introductory linguistics, phonetics, and statistics among them—and the courses below describe complementary additions I am equipped to build and lead.

General Education / Introductory Undergraduate

Introduction to Linguistics

This foundational survey introduces the core subfields—phonetics, phonology, morphology, syntax, semantics, and language in society—to a broad audience, including many students for whom it will be their only formal study of language. I treat it as an entry point into scientific reasoning as much as into linguistics: students learn to observe patterns, form and test generalizations, and recognize the systematic structure beneath everyday speech. Drawing on examples from the linguistic diversity students themselves bring to the room, I aim to leave every student—whether or not they continue in the field—better able to reason about language and the claims people make about it.

Introductory Undergraduate

Language and Mind

As the instructor of record for an undergraduate course in language acquisition aimed at language-science and cognitive-science students, I am well prepared to teach this broad entry point into the scientific study of language for students with no prior background in linguistics or cognitive science. Drawing on accessible readings from psycholinguistics, cognitive psychology, and the language sciences, the course introduces core questions: How do we recognize spoken words in real time? How do we understand sentences? How does language vary across individuals and communities, and what does that variability tell us about the mind? It is designed to reach a wide undergraduate audience, including students from psychology, cognitive science, and linguistics, while using empirical findings as a window into scientific reasoning more broadly. By the end of the course, students should be able to read and critically evaluate a primary research article in the language sciences.

Undergraduate

Introductory Acoustic Phonetics

This course introduces students to the physical properties of speech sounds from an acoustic perspective. My laboratory experience in acoustic analysis—including supervising acoustic extraction and Praat-based measurement—together with leading discussion sections in phonology, prepares me to teach this material with a strong, practical focus on empirical methods, adapted to an undergraduate audience. I will use the textbook Acoustic and Auditory Phonetics by Keith Johnson as a guide, and incorporate laboratory sessions where students gain practical experience using Praat to record, visualize, and conduct quantitative acoustic analyses of human speech.

Advanced Undergraduate or Graduate Seminar

Current Topics in Speech Perception

This seminar explores the cognitive mechanisms underlying spoken language processing, building on my experience as a teaching assistant for an upper-level speech-science seminar. I can offer it at either the undergraduate or graduate level, tailoring the curriculum to the audience. For undergraduates, the course focuses on foundational concepts using accessible reviews and brief empirical papers, supported by two to three laboratory sessions in which students use Praat to visualize phenomena such as categorical perception. For graduate students, the syllabus shifts toward critical analysis of theoretical debates and classic literature. Drawing on my own research in speech adaptation and experience-based plasticity, I aim to equip students with the theoretical and methodological tools necessary to investigate how the perceptual system accommodates variability in everyday listening situations.

Undergraduate or Graduate

Statistics for Language Science

This course addresses a critical gap in many curricula: applying statistical theory to the specific constraints of language-science data. Generic social-science statistics courses provide a foundation but often lack immediate relevance to language research. Drawing directly on my experience as a teaching assistant for probability and statistics for the social sciences, where I led the applied lab component, I have designed this course to bridge that gap, using open-access textbooks and current methodological papers to teach reproducible data analysis. Students will work in R with both real and simulated datasets, learning to handle the complex, nested data structures (e.g., items within conditions) that are typical of psycholinguistic experiments.

Undergraduate or Graduate

Experimental Methods in Language Science

Given my background in behavioral research and my experience mentoring undergraduate teams through full research projects, this course provides a practicum in experimental design. Unlike a pure statistics course, it focuses on the architectural logic of research—selecting paradigms, counterbalancing stimuli, ensuring internal validity, and adhering to Open Science principles—and is structured around the full lifecycle of a reproducible project, from preregistering hypotheses on the Open Science Framework (OSF) to the final release of anonymized data and analysis scripts. I can offer it at either level: an undergraduate version centers on the hands-on practicum, guiding students through their first end-to-end study, while a graduate version adds the vertical mentorship described above, pairing graduate mentors with undergraduate teams on capstone projects. That structure gives undergraduates authentic research experience, from piloting to “sanity checking” data, while actively cultivating the mentoring and leadership skills of the graduate students.

Across all of these settings, my goal is the one I began with: to treat learning as something students do rather than something done to them. Whether in a large introductory lecture or a one-on-one mentoring relationship, I want students to leave not only knowing more about language and the mind, but more confident in their own capacity to ask a question, gather evidence, and revise what they thought they knew. For students who arrive unsure whether research is for someone like them—and for the many who will never become researchers but will spend their lives weighing claims that deserve scrutiny—I regard that confidence, and that habit of looking to the evidence, as the most durable things I can help them build. I would be glad to bring this approach to your department.
