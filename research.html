Research

My research seeks to understand the cognitive and neural mechanisms underlying human speech processing. Specifically, I am interested in (1) if and how resilient speech learning can be in the context of native and nonnative communication; (2) the neural mechanisms that support such plasticity in rapid and long-term learning; and (3) if and how being bilingual and/or multilingual may influence the learning process.

Research projects

Measurement

Developing a Multi-scale Toolkit to Quantify Linguistic Experience

Macro-level: Stable, community-level experience

Atlas-CH — Successful language comprehension requires listeners to rapidly adapt to linguistic variability, including accented speech. While a growing body of research suggests that long-term exposure to diverse linguistic input can facilitate such adaptation, characterizing the real-world experience remains a challenge. We introduce a novel geolocation-based metric that quantifies local linguistic diversity using US Census data at the zip code level. We report an initial application of this method in a large-scale online perceptual study (N=647) of monolingual English speakers across the United States, showing that our census-based metric predicted both subjective familiarity with the accent and perceptual adaptation during the first few moments of exposure. Crucially, adaptation was predicted by the prevalence of Chinese-accented speech in participants' local environment, but not by the prevalence of other accents (e.g., Spanish), suggesting accent-dependent facilitation.

Atlas-SP — Extending the Accent Atlas approach to Spanish-accented English.

Micro-level: Dynamic, individualized experience

SNAP-Heritage-Mandarin — An Experience Sampling Method (ESM) protocol capturing real-time ambient overhearing in heritage Mandarin-English bilinguals.

Perception

Category-specific Adaptation to Nonnative Accents

Hearing Beyond Categories: General Adaptation to Nonnative Speech (CogSci 2025) — Listeners demonstrate remarkable adaptability in speech perception, rapidly improving their recognition of non-native accented speech after brief exposure. This study examines whether such adaptation reflects category-specific adjustments or a broader relaxation in processing unfamiliar accented categories. Using a pretest-exposure-posttest paradigm, we conducted two experiments to investigate how native English listeners' perception of the Mandarin-accented /θ/-/s/ contrast changed as a function of accent exposure.

Processing

Error Detection in Nonnative Speech

Investigating how attentional resources are allocated when speech has multiple sources of deviation across different linguistic domains.
