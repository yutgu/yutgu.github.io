# Research Statement

I am a cognitive scientist studying how human listeners adapt to the speech they encounter. Speech is never the same twice—regional dialects, nonnative accents, individual voices—each presents a moving acoustic target. And yet listeners adapt quickly, often within minutes. My research asks what kind of learning makes this possible, and how the structure of a listener's experience drives it. By "experience," I mean two distinct but interacting things: the moment-to-moment task environment of a specific acoustic encounter, and the accumulated history of years of ambient exposure that a listener brings into that encounter. My research program spans three converging lines: (1) using Mandarin-accented English as a test case to ask what actually changes inside a listener when they adapt to an unfamiliar accent—their perceptual representations, or something later in the processing stream; (2) developing, as the core of my dissertation, a new toolkit for measuring linguistic experience itself, moving beyond retrospective self-report to capture real-world exposure as a continuous, dynamic variable; and (3) extending this plasticity framework from perception to production, asking how bilingual experience reshapes real-time speaker coordination.

## Section 1: The Cognitive Mechanisms of Accent Adaptation

When a listener gets better at understanding an unfamiliar accent, where in the processing stream has that improvement actually occurred? The behavioral signature—more accurate word recognition, fewer misperceptions—looks the same regardless of where the change sits, which makes the question genuinely difficult to answer. Two broad possibilities organize the field. Suppose a Mandarin-accented talker says something that sounds like *ausor*. One possibility is that listeners adjust at the **lexical level**: they still hear the [s] sound exactly as before, but become more willing to accept that pronunciation as the word *author*. The other is that they adjust earlier, at the **pre-lexical level**, where acoustic input is mapped onto phonetic categories—they actually come to hear the sound as more /θ/-like. Within this pre-lexical possibility, two mechanisms are further distinct: listeners may change the way they map acoustic cues onto categories (genuinely learning that, for this talker, what sounded like [s] should be heard as /θ/), or they may simply shift a decision bias at the categorization stage (preferring to respond /θ/ overall, without changing how the cues themselves are interpreted). Distinguishing these accounts matters because each implies a different theory of what conversation with an unfamiliar talker actually does to a listener. My first line of research uses the /θ/–/s/ contrast in Mandarin-accented English as a test case. /θ/ is absent from Mandarin's phoneme inventory, and L1-Mandarin speakers commonly produce English /θ/ in acoustically variable ways that overlap with /s/—the kind of input that should require genuine adaptation. The three subsections below describe how I (1) isolate the cognitive locus of this learning, (2) characterize the acoustic input that drives it, and (3) measure the cognitive demands that constrain it.

### 1a. Where in the processing stream does adaptation occur?

The natural-accent adaptation literature has, until recently, relied almost entirely on tasks that require explicit lexical access—transcription, cross-modal priming, lexical decision. This creates a confound: each of these tasks engages the lexical threshold described above, making it impossible to tell whether an apparent improvement reflects a genuine perceptual change or merely a more lenient decision rule. To break this confound, my collaborators and I designed a series of Pretest–Exposure–Posttest experiments with two methodological innovations distinguishing our work from prior efforts (Gu, 2025; Gu, 2026).

The first innovation is a **bidirectional lexical biasing design**. Participants were randomly assigned to either a /θ/-biased context—where the Mandarin-accented talker produced items in which the /θ/ pronunciation yielded a real English word and the /s/-substituted version did not (e.g., *author* vs. \*ausor)—or a mirrored /s/-biased context (e.g., *decide* vs. \*dethide). The logic is one of diagnosis. If listeners were simply lowering their threshold for accepting one specific accented sound as a particular word—say, becoming more willing to hear \*seory as *theory* given the accent context—we would see a shift in only the /θ/-biased condition. Opposite-direction shifts in both conditions, by contrast, would indicate a more general mechanism that tracks whichever lexical context is currently in play—a richer pattern that earlier unidirectional designs (notably Zheng & Samuel, 2020) could not distinguish from the simpler one-sided shift.

Across two initial experiments, listeners showed robust opposite-direction shifts on a posttest aligned with their assigned bias, ruling out the one-sided account. But two further findings complicated the picture. First, a non-linguistic control condition replacing the accent exposure with pure tones produced a statistically indistinguishable shift, suggesting the accent exposure itself was not the primary driver. Second, fine-grained timecourse analyses revealed that the bidirectional divergence had already begun within the brief pre-test block itself, before any dedicated exposure phase. Together, these results overturn a default assumption in the field: that a dedicated exposure phase is what drives adaptation. In our paradigm, the lexical-decision task itself is acting as the learning context. This is theoretically important on its own—it suggests that any task requiring lexical access provides a teaching signal, and that the conventional boundary between "exposure" and "test" in adaptation studies is less clean than the standard paradigm assumes.

This realization motivated the critical third experiment, where we moved the lexical-decision task (alongside a counterbalanced category-identification task) into the exposure phase, and introduced a phonetic categorization probe—on a minimal-pair *theme*–*seem* continuum—as the pre- and posttest measure. The probe is diagnostic because both response options are real English words: the lexical-level decision is neutralized, so any pre-to-post shift cannot be attributed to a lexical-level change alone. Listeners showed a robust bidirectional shift aligned with their assigned bias, demonstrating that at least some of the adaptation is occurring at or before the phonetic categorization stage. The lexical-level account, on its own, cannot explain the data.

What remains open—and what I am currently pursuing through computational modeling—is which of the two pre-lexical mechanisms described above is actually doing the work: a genuine update to the cue-to-category mapping, or a shift in the pre-lexical decision bias. Both predict the same proportion of /θ/ responses and so cannot be distinguished by the behavioral data alone. My next step is to use trial-level Bayesian belief-updating models to formally tease these two mechanisms apart, simulating how each would predict the trial-by-trial response patterns in our existing data. This modeling pipeline will also support a longitudinal extension I plan as part of my independent program: tracking whether these pre-lexical shifts compound across multi-session exposure or plateau quickly.

### 1b. What acoustic input drives this adaptation?

The pre-lexical changes documented in 1a presuppose something to learn in the acoustic signal. This raises a question the natural-accent literature has rarely addressed directly: when L1-English listeners struggle with a Mandarin-accented /θ/, is it because the talker has collapsed the contrast in production, or because the talker preserves it along acoustic dimensions L1-English listeners don't typically weight? The Interlanguage Speech Intelligibility Benefit hypothesis offers a test: listeners who share the talker's L1 should succeed where naive L1-English listeners fail, because they share the speaker's phonetic space.

With my collaborator Jacob Bower, I tested this directly using L1-Mandarin listeners' forced-choice identification of the same talker's /θ/–/s/ contrast in real-word and nonword frames (Bower et al., 2026). Two findings emerged, both complicating the standard story. First, L1-Mandarin listeners *do* show acoustic sensitivity to the contrast: they reliably distinguish /θ/-tokens from /s/-tokens above chance, with a slight baseline tilt toward /θ/ rather than the bias toward /s/ that an L1-substitution account would predict. So the talker is producing an acoustic distinction, and listeners who share her L1 can detect it. But the lexical frame exerts an even larger effect on their responses than the acoustic signal does, particularly on nonwords where the acoustic signal alone is most ambiguous. And in preliminary follow-up data using the same *theme*–*seem* continuum from 1a, L1-English listeners show a *steeper* categorization function than L1-Mandarin listeners do. Sharing the talker's L1 does not, in this case, yield more categorical perception.

Together, these findings restructure the question 1a was built on. The communication challenge here is not that the signal is absent, nor that it requires the listener's own L1 to be parsed; it is that the acoustic information is *detectable but does not enforce categorical decisions*, leaving listeners—regardless of L1 background—to recruit lexical context to compensate. The substantial individual differences in this compensation point to a question I will pursue in future work: what determines how much an individual listener draws on lexical context when the acoustic signal is weak, and does prolonged exposure to native English shift that weighting? An immediate follow-up I am developing compares U.S.-resident L1-Mandarin learners with L1-Mandarin learners still in China.

### 1c. How do processing demands constrain adaptation?

Sections 1a and 1b together suggest that adapting to a nonnative accent is, at root, a problem of compensating for an impoverished or ambiguous acoustic signal—drawing on lexical context, attentional focus, and gradual perceptual restructuring. This is cognitively expensive work, raising a complementary question: what happens to other aspects of language processing when so much of the listener's cognitive budget is going into accent compensation? Using a speech error detection paradigm, my collaborators and I examined how listeners detect different kinds of errors—phonological (e.g., *will* → *wheel*) versus syntactic or lexical (e.g., *do* → *make*)—in Spanish-accented English (Gu, 2026; Hsu, 2025).

Two dissociations emerged. First, with continued exposure, non-phonological errors became easier to detect while phonological errors did not, suggesting that phonological compensation is the bottleneck that eases first. Second, when both error types co-occurred, detection of either was impaired, consistent with symmetric competition over a shared processing pool. Together with 1a and 1b, these findings characterize nonnative speech processing as a heavily resource-sensitive system: the depth of adaptation is jointly constrained by what cognitive resources remain after basic signal recovery. This motivates a natural clinical extension I plan to pursue: listeners with hearing loss expend significant effort recovering native speech even before any accent challenge, and an unfamiliar accent on top of that may deplete the resources required for the meaning-driven engagement that, as 1a suggests, is essential for genuine pre-lexical learning—with direct implications for targeted listening training in aging and hearing-impaired populations.

## Section 2: Quantifying the Structure of Linguistic Experience (Dissertation)

The rapid shifts described in Section 1 do not occur in a vacuum. They unfold against a baseline accumulating over a listener's lifetime of ambient exposure—exposure that, decades of statistical learning research tells us, leaves measurable perceptual traces even when listeners are not actively attending to the input (Saffran et al., 1996; Au et al., 2002). This creates a methodological paradox: the more implicit a learning mechanism is, the less accessible it is to the retrospective self-report instruments the field typically uses to measure linguistic background. My dissertation addresses this gap by developing a triangulated toolkit that operationalizes real-world linguistic experience as a continuous, multi-timescale variable. The central conceptual move is to distinguish two timescales. **Linguistic climate** is the stable, accumulated exposure history a listener has internalized over years. **Linguistic weather** is the dynamic, fluctuating input of a listener's immediate, day-to-day environment. Climate provides the baseline; weather provides the moment-to-moment perturbations. The two should interact, but no existing instrument measures both. My dissertation develops three coordinated tools: (i) a census-based community language prevalence index at the zip-code level; (ii) a perception-focused exposure questionnaire that explicitly distinguishes accent exposure from general language use; and (iii) an experience-sampling protocol that captures real-time ambient encounters via mobile survey.

### 2a. Climate: Community language prevalence

The census-based index quantifies the density of target-language speakers in a listener's residential zip code, derived from American Community Survey data. Because these residents likely communicate in English with phonological influences from their home language, the metric serves as an objective proxy for the ambient accent exposure a naive listener accumulates over years of living in their community—independent of self-report. In prior work (Gu, 2026), this index successfully predicted native-English monolinguals' baseline processing of Chinese-accented speech in a sample of 647 listeners. Crucially, the effect was accent-specific: Spanish language prevalence did not predict Chinese-accent processing, ruling out a generic "diversity exposure" explanation. Zip-code-level metrics also captured variance that coarser state-level measures missed, suggesting that previously null effects in the literature may reflect insufficient measurement resolution. The ongoing extension (*N* ≈ 650) tests generalization to Spanish-accented English while embedding the climate measure within a short-term laboratory adaptation paradigm, asking whether macro-level lifetime exposure and micro-level laboratory exposure operate independently, additively, or interactively.

### 2b. Weather: Real-time experience sampling

The experience-based sampling protocol is administered via smartphone over a 7-day period, with three stratified daily pings sampling real-time linguistic encounters. Critically, it distinguishes active listening from passive overhearing—a theoretical distinction that retrospective surveys conflate, but which matters because ambient overhearing leaves lasting perceptual traces even without comprehension demands (Au et al., 2002). One project applies the full toolkit to heritage Mandarin and Spanish speakers, who constitute a natural experiment on the relationship between perceptual input and linguistic plasticity: due to the dominance shift characteristic of heritage development (Polinsky, 2019), a heritage speaker's early climate (rich home exposure) often dissociates dramatically from their current weather (a predominantly English environment), and heritage speakers exhibit massive within-group variability tied to the amount and quality of their input.

My future independent program will use this toolkit to establish three claims. First, that early passive home input confers a lasting perceptual advantage that persists despite a later shift in current exposure—demonstrating long-term traces from purely ambient overhearing. Second, that the temporal structure of moment-to-moment overhearing in the present predicts laboratory adaptation independently of climate. Third, that climate and weather interact: ambient daily exposure facilitates rapid adaptation specifically in listeners equipped with strong foundational home input. The toolkit also positions me to track environmental transitions in real time—for instance, whether a heritage speaker's processing advantage diminishes after leaving a heritage-language-dominant home environment, and whether it can be rapidly restored upon return.

## Section 3: Experience-Dependent Flexibility in Bilingual Production

Beyond perception, my interest in experience-based plasticity extends to how bilingual experience shapes real-time production. In collaborative work (Song, 2023), I examined lexical entrainment in Mandarin-English bilinguals—the tendency for interlocutors to converge on shared lexical forms during dialogue. Viewed through an information-theoretic lens, this convergence is a form of rapid, context-dependent adaptation that optimizes speaker coordination and meaning inference. The findings extend the plasticity framework from perception to production, and provide the foundation for an integrative project I plan as part of my independent program: using the experience-sampling metrics from Section 2 to predict production-side outcomes, closing the loop between what a bilingual listener has been hearing in the wild and what they produce in spontaneous conversation.

## Synthesis and Future Directions

Taken together, these three lines share a single throughline: that the structure of a listener's experience—both the immediate task environment and the accumulated history of ambient exposure—is the engine of perceptual and communicative plasticity. My research program brings together highly controlled behavioral experimentation, computational modeling, fine-grained acoustic analysis, and psychometric instrument development. This methodological breadth lets me move flexibly between tightly controlled laboratory work and large-scale measurement of linguistic experience in the wild, and it positions my independent program to bridge speech science, sociophonetic variation, and cognitive modeling. As I build an independent lab, I aim to connect the controlled study of adaptation with the messy reality of how listeners accumulate and deploy linguistic experience across the lifespan—work that invites collaboration across phonetics, psycholinguistics, sociolinguistics, and the cognitive sciences. A collaborative orientation grounded in open science is central to how I work: my data pipelines, analysis scripts, and experimental repositories are designed from the outset to be transparent and reusable.
