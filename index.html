Hello, welcome :)

I am a fourth-year PhD candidate working with Xin Xie in the Department of Language Science at the University of California, Irvine.

My research asks how linguistic experience shapes the plasticity of human speech and language processing. As listeners, we constantly encounter variation — regional dialects, nonnative accents, unfamiliar voices — and yet we adapt, often within minutes. What kind of learning makes this possible, and how does the structure of a listener's experience drive it?

I pursue this question along three converging lines. First, I run behavioral experiments and computational models that disentangle the cognitive mechanisms of accent adaptation — teasing apart genuine changes in perceptual representations from shifts in decision-making strategy. Second, as the core of my dissertation, I build new tools to measure linguistic experience itself, distinguishing the stable linguistic climate a listener accumulates over years from the dynamic, day-to-day weather of their immediate environment. Third, I extend this plasticity framework from perception to production, asking how bilingual experience reshapes real-time speaker coordination.
